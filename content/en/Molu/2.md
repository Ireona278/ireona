---
title: How utterly suffocating this is
---
March 25, 2026. I want to express how I feel right now through visible words.

I feel incredibly distracted. My nerves are completely on edge. I don't particularly like being in this state, but looking at my environment, it seems I have no choice but to be this way.

This is how I see it: whenever I try to move from this state toward a sense of calm, the surrounding noises catch onto my senses and set my nerves on edge again. Even when I try to escape this hyper-sensitized state, the ambient sounds pull me back into it. Then, my mood worsens. It feels as though I have to maintain this state of high tension just to make the surrounding noises slightly less unbearable?

For example, if I try to do nothing—no smartphone, no computer—and attempt something like meditation in my current home, the sound of the elevator operating in the building immediately begins to scrape at my nerves. Every time I hear it, I feel a throbbing sensation in my brain. The elevator noise is incredibly loud. It echoes throughout the entire house. It’s audible no matter which room I’m in, even with the doors closed. It’s like the sound of someone scratching a chalkboard.

And the elevator isn't everything. The moment I try to settle down, the sound of cars racing outside begins to grate on me, as does the sound of neighbors moving around. Even when I want to find peace in an environment free from repulsive sounds, I simply cannot. Such a space is not provided to me right now.

I live in what is commonly called an 'apartment.' The elevator noise seems to be the worst of it. So, I’m thinking about mentioning this to the management office. I haven't spoken to them yet, but I believe there’s a very high probability that saying something won't do any good.

There are certain responses I expect to hear, with a high degree of certainty, if I do speak up. I want to record them here. For instance, things like: 'No one else is complaining, so why are you the only one being like this?'

As I mentioned before, it does seem true that if I stay in a state of constant tension, I don’t hear things as clearly. If I keep my nerves frayed and stay frantically active, I can eventually fall asleep. But sleeping like that makes me wonder if this is the right way to live. Yesterday was exactly like that. As I write this, I haven't been awake for very long. It’s 7:57 AM. I’m noting the time as I write this part.

To describe how I feel upon waking up—I feel a bit dazed. My head aches slightly. Even while I was asleep, those sounds I mentioned must have been occurring incessantly. Just because I don't remember them doesn't mean I believe they had no effect on my body.

Here are a few more predicted responses if I bring up the elevator to the management office: 'We don't have the budget,' or 'Even if we fix it, what about the inconvenience to the residents during the construction?' Things like that.

Some might suggest, 'Why don't you just move?' It’s not that I haven't thought about it. But when I think about moving, these thoughts come to mind:

'Even if I move, can I be sure the environment there will be okay? What if it’s even worse than here?' 'Do I have enough financial leeway to move? The thought of the expenses is too burdensome.'

How utterly suffocating this is.

Then there’s the matter of 'fine dust.' According to the Korea Meteorological Administration and weather channels, the PM10 concentration is around **$89\mu g/m^3$** and PM2.5 is around **$70\mu g/m^3$**. I wonder if this is also one of the reasons why it’s hard for me to calm down and why my nerves are so on edge. It seems the fine dust levels were like this all day yesterday, too. Reaching these levels has become such a daily occurrence. Isn't this something that even moving can't solve?

Talking to the management office is simply a way of doing _something_ because staying idle is too frustrating. I don't expect them to solve anything. My mindset is just: 'Let’s at least hear what they have to say.' Even if the reaction is predictable... well, you never know.
